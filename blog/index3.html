I haven't done any dissertation work for the past two weeks. Normally I'd be okay with this. I tend to work 7-days a week year-round, so the occasional break feels acceptable. But as of today I'm feeling a strange kind of guilt about not working. It's not enough to get me to start working again but definitely enough to prevent me from doing the other stuff I enjoy. I'm left sitting at my desk, shuffling my seriously worn-out deck of cards, and waiting for my brain to start tickling again.

The problem is that this time, I'm feeling a subtle amount of dread that I can't quite place. I am used to saying so ironically, but today it has hit me in the teeth with the genuine sting of a stepped-on rake: writing this PhD dissertation is the most soul-sucking, tedious, joyless thing ever - it's on the level of alphabetizing the files in the filing cabinets of the dusty and poorly lit storage room at your doctor's office - it connects me with nothing and no one at all, in fact it's the most alienating thing I've ever done.

It's really worrisome. It feels like the re-emergence of a thought-pattern I thought I'd put to rest: the one where I obsess over something, burnout or get bored, realize it's not for me, change career path, repeat - all in the name of authentic living! It was me noticing this pattern that in part led me to commit so intently to pursuing a PhD. One modest existential crisis later, I realized that I had accomplished nothing with this MO. So I got out of the rut by committing myself to a pursuit that seemed larger than me, something I thought worthwhile. And since then this pursuit has been the all-supporting backdrop to my life, enabling me to manage the other perhaps more minor battles with authenticity that I've faced.

That's the dread then, it's the terrifying possibility that what I thought was a permanent fixture - something that has kept me grounded - is finally fading away: What if this is a burnout warranting a total change of course? What if this career path was a mistake after all? (Is this what a breakup feels like?)

Stating this out loud has made me realize how ridiculous the sentiment is. It has reminded me that almost every moment along this journey has been one filled with excitement, discoveries, epiphanies, about society, humanity, and history and even about myself. For the past five years, it has been true for me without question that I was destined for this. Isn't that enough to justify staying the course?

Maybe it's framing with respect to authenticity that's what's screwing me over. Maybe it's time for some tough love. Here's a thought: this tedium that's now pressing itself into my face is just the price I have to pay for the privilege of pursuing this degree. What I am truly confronting now - this lull - is the fact that my job is take all these wonderful discoveries and weave them together into a coherent, readable text that other people can learn from. It's true that it's tedious but it is required - I should share my findings with others, I owe it to them - otherwise what am I to them? Probably just a selfish blob leeching off the system. Or the worst thing of all: a mere consumer. (High pitched scream in the distance.)

So this isn't a battle with authenticity. Or maybe it's a need to reconfigure the basis for authenticity. The fact of the matter is that if I am inferring from my boredom that I've chosen the wrong path because the correct - authentic - one could never make me feel like this then I am making a huge selfish mistake. Wait a minute - have I been doing this my whole life? Have I been masquerading my lack of productivity, my constant changes in direction, as a quest for authenticity, when it's really an unconscious drive to absorb the world while contributing nothing?

That seems too harsh. I can admit that authenticity is important to me and has driven me towards this awesome fulfilling career while also admitting I'm part of a community, one that I care about and need to care for. In fact, this realization helps a lot: I now have a new reason to share my findings - to share any of my creations really - that doesn't come from the mere selfish desire to be recognized as a creator. I have a duty to share which stems from a desire to not be selfish - a duty to contribute and not merely consume.

I need to think this over more. One thing that sticks is that if I'm always dipping my toe in a ton of different hobbies without giving back, then I'm really nothing good for my community.

So this is something new to latch onto: I should ground my motivation to finish the things I start in duty to share my creations and to contribute.
